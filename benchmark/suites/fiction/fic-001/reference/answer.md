Corven lit the first lamp of his round the way he had lit thirty years of first lamps: with the long brass wand held level, the flame kissed to the wick, and a small nod, as if to a neighbor who had waited up for him. The glass caught and steadied. Warm light pushed a clean circle onto the cobbles, and beyond that circle the dark drew back the way a dog backs off a raised hand.

The Hollow was patient tonight. He could feel it the way he felt weather in his knees. On the bad nights it crowded the wall, thick as breath on cold glass, and the lamps hissed as though something leaned against their light. On the quiet nights it only waited. This was a waiting night. He did not trust waiting nights. Waiting was what a thing did when it had already decided how the evening would end.

He worked south along the wall. Second lamp, third. His hands knew the wands and the wicks and the little stubborn one at the fourth post that always wanted coaxing. Thirty years had worn the round into him until it was less a task than a shape his body made after dark. Light the lamp. A lit lamp is a wall. Move on. Leave nothing dark, because a dark lamp is a door, and doors in Ashmere only opened one way.

He had buried men who forgot that. Not many. The town did not keep lamplighters who forgot.

At the seventh post he stopped, because the seventh post was where the ground began its slow rise toward the paupers' field, and because he could hear singing.

It was thin and off the pitch, a child's voice worrying at a tune that had no business out here past the wall's shadow. He knew the tune. He had known the voice, once, before the fever took it, before he had carried a box no heavier than a sack of kindling out to the field and set it in the dark with his own hands because that was the law and he was a lawful man.

"Papa," the voice said, from beyond the seventh lamp. "You always come this way."

Corven did not answer it. He had been warned about answering. The Hollow wore the dead like a coat and used their voices like keys, and a man who answered had already turned the lock halfway. He lifted the wand. The seventh wick waited, cold and clean.

"It's dark out here," she said. "You know how I am about the dark."

He did know. He knew she had slept with a rushlight until she was six, that she had made him check the corners, that she had held his two fingers because his whole hand was too big to be frightened of anything. He knew the thing beyond the lamp could not know these things, and he knew, with the cold clarity of a man reading his own hand, that it did not need to. It only needed him to want them true.

"Light it," she said, "and then I can't see you. The light's so bright, Papa. It puts you all in shadow."

That was the truth of it, and the truth was the cruelest key of all. A lit lamp was a wall, and a wall has two sides, and for thirty years he had stood on the bright side and let the dark keep everything it took. He had lit this lamp four hundred times since the fever. He had never once let himself stand still in front of it and listen.

His thumb found the striker. The flame trembled at the end of the wand, small and dutiful and waiting to be a wall.

"Are you cold?" he asked. His voice came out cracked from disuse; he had not spoken since the tavern.

A silence. Then: "Not if you come."

He thought about the round. Nineteen lamps still south of him, and the whole eastern arc after, and the boys who would find the seventh dark at dawn and know a door had stood open all night, and the ledger where his name had been the only name for three decades, clean, exact, unbroken. He thought about what came through open doors. He had seen the aftermath twice and helped bury both. He was not a fool and he was not brave and he did not, even now, think of himself as anything but a tired man with a wand.

He lowered the wand.

He pinched the flame out between two wet fingers, the way he had taught her to kill a candle so the smoke wouldn't sting, and he set the wand down against the post with the care of a man laying down a tool he would not need again. The seventh lamp stood cold. Unlit. A door.

The dark past it did not rush. It came the way she used to come down the stairs when she was trying to be quiet and failing, one careful foot and then the giving-up run. Cold moved over the cobbles and touched his boots. He did not step back into the circle of the sixth lamp, though it was three strides behind him and still burning and still a wall.

"There you are," he said, and opened his big frightening hand, and held out two fingers.
